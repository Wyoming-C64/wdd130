Early Works

Although I had been alive only six years, my world drastically changed when my mom received a birthday gift in September 1982... A home computer system.

The Commodore 64 was released only a month before but had been hyped up since January. It featured 64k of RAM, 320x200x16 bitmapped graphics, programmable hardware "sprites", 3-channel synthesized audio, and an MSRP roughly half that of its competitors. With all of that in its favor, the Commodore 64 set the Guinness World Record for Best-selling Desktop Computer.

I watched my parents start up the computer and go through the Commodore 64 User’s Guide to learn how to use the computer. The majority of the manual introduces new owners to the BASIC programming language and provides sample programs that showcase many of the computer’s features.

Being the precocious child I was, I thought, I can do that too, and opened the User’s Guide to learn for myself. By the time I completed elementary school, I had learned enough to be able to write a small, but useful application to inventory my model trains.

The Programs

Job - 1982

The very first program I wrote on my own and saved to disk.

Screen Change - 1983

A small program that changes the background color based on user input.

Prediction - 1985

The psychic computer asks your name and gives a prediction.

Robeep - 1985

The computer randomly chooses letters until it spells a word. Beeping included.

Gypsy Fortune - 1986

Unlike "Prediction", this fortune teller is not targeted at anyone specific for certain fates. The program asks for your name only as a courtesy, and the fortunes are all completely random.

E-Bits! - 1987-1988

My epic game program idea. Unfortunately, I have not been able to recover the entire program... yet.

Here Come Da Judge! - 1988-1989

The first useful application I wrote for my dad, the optometrist. Patients were behind on paying him. Here's a handy program to figure out how much they owe in late fees and other charges.

Railroad Roster - 1991

By now, the Commodore 64 was becoming a thing of the past as more people were settling with either IBM PC DOS-compatible or Apple Macintosh computers as their home computer.

This was the last big program I wrote and mostly completed on our Commodore 64 before it was put in storage. I wanted a database to keep track of all of my HO scale model trains, so I decided to build one from scratch.
